JPS officials say the program included house-to-house assessments, electrical inspections, community mapping, and education campaigns focused on electricity safety, energy conservation, and building a payment culture. As part of the transition, more than 200 now-new prepaid customers received electricity credits over six months to support the move to legal service. Residents say the project has brought greater stability, reduced damage, and removed the pressure of paying individuals to tamper with power lines. Community leaders have welcomed the development, describing legal access to electricity as a step towards safer homes, stronger small businesses, and improved quality of life.
