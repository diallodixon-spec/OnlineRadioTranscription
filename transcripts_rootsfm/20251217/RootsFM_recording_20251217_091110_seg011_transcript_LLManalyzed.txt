At the beginning of the English rule, the parish was one of the poorest. It had no towns, few heat habitats, and little commerce except for the exported lard. However, after the treaty with the Maroons in 1739, St. James became one of the most important sugar-producing parishes, and only more than 150 ships arrived in Montego Bay, bringing slaves and supplies and taking sugar.
