Interviews for time range 17:30 - 17:59
======================================================================

**Interview 1: Energy Minister Daryl Vaz and Opposition Leader Mark Golding**

In a recent interview, Energy Minister Daryl Vaz and Opposition Leader Mark Golding discussed the government's plan to loan $150 million to the Jamaica Public Service Company (JPS) to restore electricity island-wide. Minister Vaz stated, "We are prepared to release the $150 million tonight if it means that the people are going to get back light sooner than later." However, Opposition Leader Mark Golding raised concerns about the funding, asking, "So to summarize, one is the timeline for JPS to fund the $200 million gap, and whether you would ensure that that funding is in place before you expose our $150 million."

**Interview 2: Erd Moxham and Dion**

In an interview with Erd Moxham, special assignments editor, and Dion, they discussed the impact of Hurricane Melissa on the country. Erd Moxham shared his personal experience, stating, "Melissa just took over and has dominated our lives since October 28, so much so that there's hardly anything else that we have had the opportunity or the freedom to contemplate." Dion added, "I think it's important to also note that one area of consensus between the two dominant political parties was in respect of the establishment of the zones of special operations."

**Interview 3: Arthur Hall**

Arthur Hall, editor at large at the Jamaica Observer, joined the discussion, highlighting the media's role in covering the aftermath of Hurricane Melissa. He praised the quality of reporting, stating, "I will say, and I will challenge anybody, that the quality of our reporting in radio, TV, print in Jamaica... has been absolutely fantastic." He also acknowledged the challenges faced by reporters, saying, "Some of the reporters who have been... on the radio, on the TV, in print, online, bringing them the action, the stories in relation to Melissa, have themselves been absolutely devastated or badly affected."

**Interview 4: Assistant Commissioner Calvin Allen**

Assistant Commissioner Calvin Allen discussed the four-hour standoff between a murder suspect and the police in Mandeville, Manchester. He explained, "The gunman had a dispute with his victim... to Caledonia Road, where we experience over four hours of standoff." This incident highlights the ongoing security challenges faced by the country.

**Interview 5: Justice Minister Delroy Chuck**

Justice Minister Delroy Chuck discussed the government's plan to increase penalties for murder. He stated, "The sentence, Madam Speaker, for capital murder should be life imprisonment, or if a determinate sentence is considered appropriate by the judge, 30 years. But in either case, the offender must serve 15 years." This move aims to address the country's high murder rate and provide justice for victims' families.

**Interview 6: Executive Director of Jamaicans for Justice, Michail Jackson**

Michail Jackson, Executive Director of Jamaicans for Justice, raised concerns about the potential impact of the increased penalties on children convicted of murder. He stated, "We believe that generally when it comes to child justice matters, the full discretion should be within the remit of the courts. It will worsen the prison overcrowding challenges that we have and it undermines the principle of rehabilitation and child justice." This highlights the need for a nuanced approach to addressing crime and justice in the country.
